Babri Masjid Demolition

Introduction

The Babri Masjid Demolition refers to the destruction of the Babri Masjid, a mosque in Ayodhya, Uttar Pradesh, India, on December 6, 1992. The demolition triggered widespread communal violence and had significant political and social repercussions in India.

Background and Historical Context

The Babri Masjid, built in the 16th century, was a source of religious and political contention. Hindus claimed that the mosque was built on the birthplace of Lord Ram, while Muslims asserted their right to the site. The dispute became a focal point for communal tensions and legal battles.

Demolition and Aftermath

On December 6, 1992, a large gathering of Hindu activists gathered at the disputed site and demolished the Babri Masjid. The event led to nationwide communal riots, resulting in significant loss of life and property. The demolition and its aftermath remain a contentious issue in Indian politics.

Legal Proceedings and Ayodhya Verdict

Legal battles ensued following the demolition. The Liberhan Commission was appointed to investigate the events leading to the demolition. In 2010, the Allahabad High Court delivered a verdict on the Ayodhya dispute, dividing the disputed land among Hindu and Muslim claimants. The Supreme Court of India later upheld this decision in 2019.

Social and Political Impact

The Babri Masjid Demolition had far-reaching consequences for Indian society and politics. It heightened communal tensions, leading to increased polarization and debates on secularism. The event also influenced the trajectory of Indian politics and the rise of identity-based movements.

Legacy and Lessons

The Babri Masjid Demolition continues to be a sensitive and debated topic in India. It underscores the challenges of religious pluralism and the importance of fostering communal harmony. Efforts toward reconciliation and understanding remain crucial for building a more inclusive society.
